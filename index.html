Patient Decision Aid for Parents Considering Hepatitis B Vaccination

This decision aid is to help you understand more about Hepatitis B vaccination and determine which path is right for your family.

About Hepatitis B

Hepatitis B is a serious liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus. It is spread by contact with infected blood or bodily fluids. This can happen through an infected mother passing it to her baby at birth, direct contact with an infected person where blood is passed through breaks in the skin or soft tissues, or through sex with an infected person.

For most people, hepatitis B is short-term and lasts less than six months. If the infection lasts more than six months it is known as chronic hepatitis B. Chronic hepatitis B can raise the risk of liver failure, liver cancer, and serious scarring of the liver called cirrhosis. Most people with chronic hepatitis B will not show signs of infection unless the disease progresses. Half of all people with hepatitis B in the United States do not know that they are infected.

If an infant is infected at birth, 90 out of 100 will develop a chronic hepatitis B infection. The risk of becoming infected drops to 4 out of 100 if an infant is vaccinated at birth and receives follow up treatment.

About Hepatitis B Vaccines

Vaccines that protect against the hepatitis B virus are available as single-antigen or combination antigen vaccines. Single-antigen vaccines protect against one disease, in this case hepatitis B. Combination antigen vaccines protect against multiple diseases with a single vaccine. Both single-antigen and combination antigen hepatitis B vaccine series typically consist of 3 doses given over a 6-month period to provide full protection. The protection provided by the hepatitis B vaccine is long-lasting and does not require additional booster doses beyond the primary vaccine series.

Whenever feasible, the same vaccine should be used for subsequent doses. There have been no differences in efficacy observed when one or 2 doses of hepatitis B vaccine produced by one manufacturer are followed by doses from a different manufacturer.

Clinicians can administer hepatitis B vaccine at the same time as other vaccines to children. There is no evidence that this reduces the vaccine's effectiveness.

In the United States, hepatitis B vaccines are inactivated vaccines which do NOT contain live virus and cannot cause a hepatitis B infection.
