Understanding the Decision

This decision aid is designed to help you make an informed choice about whether your newborn should receive the hepatitis B vaccine within 24 hours of birth. It is not meant to tell you what to decide — it is meant to make sure you have the information and space to decide for yourself.

ℹ️
How to use this tool Work through each section at your own pace using the Next button below. You can go back at any time. At the end, you will receive a personal summary to bring to your conversation with your healthcare provider.

As a parent, you have three options:

Vaccinate at Birth
Your baby receives the hepatitis B vaccine within 24 hours of birth.
Delay Vaccination
You choose to wait until a later visit (typically 2 months) to begin the vaccine series.
Decline Vaccination
You choose not to vaccinate your baby against hepatitis B at this time.
⚠️
These options carry different risks This decision aid will walk you through what is known about those risks so you can weigh them clearly.

This decision aid was developed as part of a graduate public health course and is based on peer-reviewed evidence and ACIP guidelines. It is not a substitute for advice from your healthcare provider.

What Is Hepatitis B?

Hepatitis B is caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) which attacks the liver. It spreads through blood and body fluids — for example, from a mother to her baby during birth, through household contact with someone who is infected, or through unsterile medical or tattoo equipment.

Many people with hepatitis B don't know they have it This is important because you cannot rely on visible signs of illness to know if someone in your household or community is a carrier. A person can have chronic hepatitis B for years without any symptoms, while still being capable of transmitting the virus to others.

If hepatitis B infection becomes chronic (long-lasting), it can lead to serious liver disease over time. Chronic hepatitis B causes ongoing inflammation in the liver, which can lead to scarring of the liver tissue, a condition called cirrhosis. In some people, severe cirrhosis can progress to liver failure. People with chronic hepatitis B also have a significantly higher risk of developing primary liver cancer compared to those without the infection. Hepatitis B is the leading cause of liver cancer worldwide. Not everyone with chronic hepatitis B will experience these complications, but the risk adds up over a lifetime.

How Could My Baby Be Exposed?

There are two main ways a newborn can be exposed to hepatitis B:

RouteWhat it meansU.S. context
Vertical (birth) Passed from birthing parent to baby during delivery Primary risk when birthing parent tests positive. The birth dose + follow-up care prevents this in most cases.
Horizontal (household / community) Passed through close contact, shared objects, or open cuts and sores Lower risk in the general U.S. population, but real in households where a carrier may not know they are infected.
🏠
What about households where the birthing parent tests negative? Babies can still be infected by HBV even if the birthing parent tests negatve. For example, before universal vaccination, U.S.-born children of immigrant birthing parents who tested negative for HBV still had hepatitis B infection rates of about 1 in 10, from household and community contact.

HBV can remain on surfaces at room temperature for up to 7 days, meaning indirect transmission through shared household items — razors, toothbrushes, nail clippers — is possible when a household member has an undiagnosed infection.

Does the Vaccine Work? Is It Safe?

How well does the birth dose work?

When given within 24 hours of birth, the hepatitis B vaccine (when combined with follow-up doses) is about 90% effective at preventing transmission to babies born to infected birthing parents. At the population level, vaccination has driven a 99% decline in acute hepatitis B cases among children and young adults since 1990.

Is the vaccine safe?

The hepatitis B vaccine has one of the most thoroughly studied safety records of any vaccine, with over 40 years of use in the United States. There are some side effects associated with the hepatitis B vaccine. If you have any questions about side effects, consult a medical professional.

Common side effects

  • Soreness or redness at the injection site (about 1 in 4 babies)
  • Mild low-grade fever
  • Fussiness or drowsiness

What the evidence shows

  • No established link to autism, autoimmune disease, or developmental delay
  • No new safety signals have been identified
  • Safe for medically stable newborns ≥2,000g

What Is the Current Policy Situation?

You may have heard that recommendations for the hepatitis B birth dose have recently changed. This section explains what happened and what it means for your decision.

📅
Since 1991 — over 30 years
Universal birth dose recommended. Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) recommended the hepatitis B vaccine for all medically stable newborns within 24 hours of birth. This policy led to a 99% decline in acute hepatitis B cases among children and young adults.
⚠️
December 5, 2025
ACIP votes to change the recommendation. A reconstituted committee moved from a universal recommendation to "shared clinical decision-making" for babies of HBsAg-negative mothers. No new safety data prompted this change. The American Academy of Pediatrics called the decision "irresponsible" and "purposely misleading."
⚖️
Early 2026
Federal court issues preliminary injunction. A judge found the reconstituted committee likely violates the Federal Advisory Committee Act (FACA) and stayed the December 2025 votes, temporarily restoring the prior universal recommendation. This is a preliminary ruling — the legal case is ongoing.
📍
Today
Situation is unresolved. The policy is actively evolving. The underlying evidence for the birth dose has not changed regardless of what the recommendation says at any given moment.
🔑
What this means for your decision No matter what the official recommendation says at the time of your baby's birth, the scientific evidence about hepatitis B risk and the effectiveness of the birth dose remains the same. This decision-aid is designed to help you decide based on that evidence and not on the current state of policy.
